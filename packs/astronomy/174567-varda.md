# 174567 Varda

**174567 Varda** (provisional designation **2003 MW12**) is a large binary trans-Neptunian object in the hot classical population of the Kuiper belt, located in the outermost region of the Solar System. Its moon, Ilmarë, was discovered in 2009.

Varda is a possible dwarf planet. Objects in the size range of 400–1000 km, such as Varda, have likely never compressed into fully solid bodies, let alone differentiated, and so are highly unlikely to be dwarf planets. However, density calculations for Varda are ambiguous. It is not clear if Varda's density is high enough to compressed into a fully solid body. Its low albedo is however consistent with a lack of the geological activity that is thought to be typical of dwarf planets.

## History

### Discovery

Varda was discovered in March 2006 by American astronomer Jeffrey A. Larsen as part of a United States Naval Academy Trident Scholar project. The discovery was made using survey images originally taken on June 21, 2003, with the 0.9-meter Spacewatch telescope at the Kitt Peak National Observatory near Tucson, Arizona.

Larsen's survey ran for 34 months and covered about 8,000 square degrees of the sky to find slow-moving trans-Neptunian objects. To detect these very far bodies, the survey used an automated computer software called SLOSUR, that processed a massive 2 terabytes of imaging data. This software used a did multiple-night detection scheme to recognize the movements as slow as 0.012 approximately arcseconds per hour, which makes Varda not being mistaken for just a background star. One of Varda's early survey detections happened by accident when astronomers lengthened the telescope's exposure time to re observe a Spacewatch near-Earth asteroid discovery named SW40E6, Varda crossed the exact same field of view. At the time, Varda was the only large object discovery made by this program and was thought to be the tenth-largest known classical Kuiper belt object at that time.

### Precoveries

When Varda's discovery was announced under the provisional designation 2003 MW12, its orbit was poorly constrained because it has only been observed a few times. This was fixed in February 2006 when astronomers used the Spacewatch II telescope to track the object again and confirm its Orbital path. This tracking allowed researchers to look for older observations, known as precovery images. And the earliest known precovery images of Varda were found on images taken at the Siding Spring Observatory on 19 March 1980, and it is more than 23 years before the official discovery. More older observational images from 1983 to 1994 were taken in Palomar Mountain, while the Near-Earth Asteroid Tracking program detected Varda on 30 May and 7 June 2002, by using faint survey images. These older observations extended Varda's observation arc to over two decades, which allowed the Minor Planet Center to calculate its orbit with way more precision.

### Further observations

Following its discovery, Varda was tracked by several sky surveys to improve its orbit calculations. From 2010 to 2015, the Pan-STARRS 1 survey at Haleakala regularly observed Varda and recorded its position. On 10 September 2018, astronomers used a non-geocentric occultation observation to collect the very precise physical characteristics and orbital characteristics of the Varda-Ilmarë system. In August 2020, it was tracked by the Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System (ATLAS) on Mauna Loa. The Zwicky Transient Facility (ZTF) at Palomar Mountain observed Varda again in May 2021, followed by tracking from the Space Surveillance Telescope in June 2022. These combined survey observations continued until they stopped at the August 2024, creating a highly accurate and very precise orbital path for Varda.

## Nomenclature

### Designation and numbering

Before receiving its official name, Varda was known by the provisional designation 2003 MW12. The Minor Planet Center (MPC) assigned it the permanent minor planet number 174567 on 22 January 2008.

### Naming

The names for Varda and its moon were announced by the MPC on January 16, 2014. The object is named after Varda, the queen of the Valar in J. R. R. Tolkien's Middle-earth writings. According to International Astronomical Union (IAU) guidelines, the discoverer can propose a name to a committee that judges whether it is suitable. Within Tolkien's legendarium, Varda functions as an "angelic power" or creator deity subordinate to the supreme God Eru Ilúvatar, responsible for shaping the stars to illuminate the world. Its moon was named Ilmarë, after a chief of the Maiar and Varda's handmaiden.

### Symbol

Because the use of planetary symbols is gone out of popularity in terms of modern astronomy, Varda never received an official symbol in scientific literature. So, there is no standard symbol for Varda used by astrologers, though Zane Stein proposed a specific star glyph for Varda ().

Astronomers have analyzed multiple stellar occultations of Varda to derive its precise size, flattening, albedo, and other physical properties. The first heavily studied occultation occurred on 10 September 2018, when Varda passed in front of a bright background star, yielding a bunch of positive chords. The evidence of an atmosphere is also investigated.

While two predicted events in mid-2022 yielded no ground duration, a series of successful occultations were tracked between 2023 and 2025 across South America, East Asia, Africa, and the Arctic. Notably, the 11 March 2023 event had a maximum predicted duration of 52.7 seconds, while campaigns on 18 March 2023 and 10 June 2024 recorded separate shadow chords for both Varda and Ilmarë. During the 24 December 2025 event, only Varda's shadow crossed Earth for 20.3 seconds, while Ilmarë missed the planet entirely. Another event was predicted for 17 June 2026 across South America, involving a 16.8 magnitude star at a velocity of 24.91 km/s with an expected geocentric distance of 44.7064 au. Future campaigns are predicted for 2 July 2027 over South Asia and 17 July 2027 over Greenland and Canada, passing a 17.0 and a 16.6 magnitude star respectively.

## Orbit and classification

### Orbital characteristics, observations and precoveries

Varda orbits the Sun at a distance of 39.5–52.7 AU once every 313.1 years (over 114,000 days; semi-major axis of 46.1 AU). Its orbit has an eccentricity of 0.14 and an inclination of 21.5° with respect to the ecliptic. As of November 2019, Varda is 47.5 AU from the Sun. It will come to perihelion around November 2096. It has been observed 321 times over 23 oppositions, with precovery images back to 1980.

### Classification

Varda is located in the classical region of the Kuiper belt 39–48 AU from the Sun, and is thus classified as a typical member of a classical Kuiper belt object (also known as a "cubewano"). The high orbital inclination of Varda makes it a dynamically "hot" member of the classical Kuiper belt. The hot classical Kuiper belt objects are believed to have been scattered by Neptune's gravitational influence during the Solar System's early history.

## Physical characteristics

Varda has a highly flattened, triaxial ellipsoid shape with body dimensions of 778 × 706 × 496 km, derived from a best-fit model with radii of 389 × 353 × 248 km. Because Earth views Varda nearly pole-on (looking straight down its short c-axis), its shape looks circular from our perspective, which hid its true elongation in earlier observations. This larger body volume means Varda's true bulk density is much higher than older estimates, rising to between 1.9 g/cm³.

Varda's long axis is directed toward its moon, Ilmarë. The chance for this alignment to happen by accident is calculated to be 2%, strongly suggesting that Varda has a triaxial ellipsoid shape. However, the published data are not able to fully constrain its three-dimensional shape.

On 10 September 2018, Varda's projected diameter was previously measured to be 766±6 km via a stellar occultation, with a projected oblateness of 0.066±0.047. The area equivalent diameter is 740 km.

The rotation period of Varda is not fully certain; past estimates include 4.76, 5.91 (the most likely value), or 7.87 hours, or double those values. The total mass of the binary system is approximately 2.675+0.048 −0.049×1020 kg. Its geometric albedo is measured at 0.099, making its surface as dark as the large plutino 208996 Achlys. However, this albedo could already be outdated since newer studies measured a smaller diameter.

The surfaces of both Varda and Ilmarë appear red in visible and near-infrared light (spectral class IR), and Ilmarë is slightly redder. Light spectrum measurements show signs of methanol ice on the surface, but no clear signs of water ice. Varda is classified as a CO 2-type ("double-dip") trans-Neptunian object.

### Non-hydrostatic equilibrium shape theory

Under the assumption that Varda is rotating synchronously once every every 5.75 days, this rotational speed is not fast enough to naturally cause its highly elongated shape under the standard hydrostatic equilibrium, this would require an even faster 4.5-hour period. So, astronomers have also considered that Varda has a non-hydrostatic equilibrium shape, theorising that the ellipticity of Varda's limb could represent a "fossil bulge" that froze into place early in its history when Varda was spinning even faster, similar to Quaoar. If Varda's interior is not very uniform, then this triaxial shape could also be held together by internal friction and uneven density distributions inside of Varda.

## Binary system

### Moon

Varda has one known satellite, **Ilmarë**, formal designation **(174567) Varda I**. Ilmarë was discovered by Keith Noll in 2009, at a separation of about 0.12 arcsec, using discovery images taken by the Hubble Space Telescope on 26 April 2009, the discovery was reported in 2011. Ilmarë has a low albedo of 0.068±0.011 and is approximately 403 km in diameter according to the ALMA study done in 2025, making it the fourth-largest known moon of a trans-Neptunian object, after Charon, Dysnomia, and Vanth. Under the assumption of a lower density of 0.7 g/cm3, which is thought to be more typical that of trans-Neptunian objects in the ~400 km size range, the Ilmarë-to-Varda mass ratio would be around 0.11:1. Even if Ilmarë has the same density as Varda at 1.15 g/cm3, the mass ratio would be relatively high at 0.16:1. This makes it one of the systems with the largest known mass ratios, with the mass ratio being quite similar to Pluto-Charon or Orcus-Vanth.

#### Orbital characteristics

Ilmarë orbits Varda at a distance of 4815±29 km and an eccentricity of 0.016±0.004 respectively. Ilmarë has an orbital period of approximately 5.750824±0.000016 d. Ilmarë's orbit has an inclination of 77.4±1.9° with respect to the ecliptic.

### Tidal evolution

Observations from a 2018 stellar occultation show that Varda's longest equatorial axis points almost directly toward its moon Ilmarë. Varda's elongated axis has a position angle of 67°±8°, while Ilmarë is located at a position angle of 60.8°±0.7°. If Varda were not tidally locked, the chance of this very close alignment happening is only around 2%. Furthermore, the large size ratio between Varda and its moon Ilmarë strongly suggests that tidal synchronization should happen really quickly, especially at the small semi-major axis of the binary, similar to the Salacia-Actaea system. This alignment is almost matches the minimum energy state expected for a tidally evolved binary system.

## Exploration concepts

A study has analyzed flyby trajectories to Varda using planetary gravity assists to reduce travel time. A study by Amanda Zangari and her team used the MAKO trajectory analysis tool to calculate two main pathways to Varda, they are: Jupiter-only pathways, A spacecraft launched between 2027 and 2031 could use a single Jupiter gravity assist to reach Varda in 10.1 to 12.3 years, depending on the rocket's power. A second Jupiter-only window opens between 2039 and 2040, giving a steady flight duration of about 10.9 years. The spacecraft would reach Varda at a distance of 40.8 to 44.0 AU from the Sun; Jupiter–Saturn pathways: A multi-planet flyby window opens between 2037 and 2040. In a modeled 2038 flight path, a spacecraft launched on November 4, 2038, would fly past Jupiter on April 17, 2040, and Saturn on December 5, 2041, before arriving at Varda on September 26, 2053. This trip takes 14.9 years.

Adding a Saturn flyby actually makes the trip to Varda longer than taking a direct path using only Jupiter. Skipping Saturn saves more than 10 years of travel time in some flights. Any future mission would also fly past Varda's moon, Ilmarë, at a high speed of about 16.3 km/s.
