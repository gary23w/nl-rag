# 1936 Winter Olympics

The **1936 Winter Olympics**, officially the **IV Olympic Winter Games** (German: *IV. Olympische Winterspiele*) and commonly known as **Garmisch-Partenkirchen 1936**, were a winter multi-sport event held from 6 to 16 February 1936 in Garmisch-Partenkirchen, Germany. They were the fourth edition of the Winter Olympic Games and the first to be held in Germany. Later the same year the country also hosted the 1936 Summer Olympics in Berlin; 1936 was the last year in which one nation staged both the Summer and Winter Games.

The Games were organised on behalf of the German League of the Reich for Physical Exercise by Karl Ritter von Halt. Staged in a Germany governed by the Nazi Party, they prompted international boycott efforts; the regime temporarily removed antisemitic signage and, under outside pressure, allowed the Jewish ice hockey player Rudi Ball to compete for Germany. Within weeks of the closing ceremony, German forces remilitarized the Rhineland.

A then-record 28 nations and 646 athletes took part. They contested 17 events across four sports, with alpine skiing making its Olympic debut. Norway led the medal table with seven gold medals. Among the standout performers were Norway's Ivar Ballangrud, who won three of the four speed-skating titles, and figure skater Sonja Henie, who took her third consecutive Olympic gold, while Great Britain upset defending champions Canada to win the ice hockey tournament.

## Host selection

At its 29th Session in Barcelona in 1931, the International Olympic Committee (IOC) awarded the 1936 Summer Olympics to Berlin. Under the rules then in force, the country hosting the Summer Games was also expected, where possible, to stage the Winter Games earlier in the same year. The Bavarian towns of Garmisch and Partenkirchen, at the foot of the Zugspitze, were put forward as hosts, and the choice was confirmed by the IOC at its 31st Session in Vienna on 7 June 1933. Adolf Hitler had become German chancellor in January that year. In preparation for the Games, the two towns were merged into a single municipality, Garmisch-Partenkirchen, on 1 January 1935.

## Organization and politics

The Games were organised by Karl Ritter von Halt, who had been appointed head of the organising committee by Reichssportführer Hans von Tschammer und Osten. According to historians Arnd Krüger and William Murray, Nazi interest in winter sport was encouraged in part by its promotion in Mussolini's Italy, where skiing had been marketed to the middle class.

Because the Games were held in a state governed by the openly antisemitic Nazi regime, several countries debated boycotting both the Winter and Summer Olympics, and some Jewish athletes faced pressure not to take part. Before foreign visitors arrived, the authorities temporarily removed antisemitic signs from the host towns and—under pressure linked to a possible American boycott—allowed Rudi Ball, the only Jewish athlete on the German team, to play in the ice hockey tournament. Shortly before the opening, IOC president Henri de Baillet-Latour reminded Hitler that the Games were to be kept free of political propaganda and conducted under the IOC's rules.

No nation boycotted the Winter Games, and the 28 that competed were the most at any Winter Olympics up to that point. The competition passed with little overt political controversy, although the Canadian team's raised-arm "Olympic salute" during the opening ceremony was mistaken by spectators for the Nazi salute.

## Venues

Six venues were used for the Games:

- Große Olympiaschanze – opening and closing ceremonies, cross-country skiing, Nordic combined and ski jumping
- Gudiberg – alpine skiing (combined slalom)
- Kreuzjoch – alpine skiing (combined downhill)
- Kreuzeck – alpine skiing (downhill finish)
- Olympia-Kunsteisstadion – figure skating and ice hockey
- Riessersee and surrounding area – bobsleigh, ice hockey and speed skating

The Große Olympiaschanze ski jump was built for the Games, and the adjoining ski stadium staged the opening and closing ceremonies, the cross-country races and the ski-jumping events.

## Finances

A financial statement compiled in April 1936 showed that both spending and income had more than doubled the organising committee's original 1933 estimates of around one million Reichsmark (RM) each. Total expenditure came to 2,618,259 RM, of which about 1,257,000 RM was spent on building and enlarging the sports venues and 1,099,000 RM on organisation and administration; the remainder covered items such as propaganda, temporary barracks and clean-up. Income totalled 2,415,369 RM, including 1,253,831 RM in grants from the Garmisch and Partenkirchen municipalities, the city of Munich, the Bavarian state and the German government, and 1,010,618 RM from ticket sales. Spending thus exceeded income by 202,890 RM, a shortfall the German government undertook to cover. Much larger sums were spent outside the committee's accounts by the state railway, post office and broadcasting service, and on a new trunk road and the partial double-tracking of the Munich–Garmisch railway.

## Attendance and logistics

The Games attracted the largest crowds of any Winter Olympics up to that point; one study judges that more people attended than at all the previous Winter Games combined. On ordinary competition days between 35,000 and 50,000 spectators were present in Garmisch-Partenkirchen, rising to about 64,000 on the first Sunday and an estimated 150,000 on the closing day, 16 February. Between 5 and 17 February the state railway carried roughly 250,000 people to and from the town and the post office a further 13,000 by bus, while about 10,000 motor vehicles were in the town on the final day. Special trains left Munich at intervals as short as ten minutes through the night, and directing the traffic on the narrow Alpine roads required several hundred police and auxiliaries.

The Games also brought communications innovations. The events received full radio coverage for the first time, with 201 broadcasts transmitted abroad totalling some 236 hours; the German post office handled around 2.7 million letters and postcards, issued commemorative stamps and operated temporary post offices at the railway station and the competition venues.

## The Games

The opening ceremony took place on 6 February 1936 before a crowd of more than 30,000. Cross-country skier Willy Bogner took the Olympic Oath on behalf of the competitors.

Alpine skiing appeared at the Olympics for the first time, contested as a combined event scoring each competitor's downhill and slalom runs; the German skiers Franz Pfnür and Christl Cranz won the men's and women's titles. A dispute between the International Ski Federation and the IOC over whether ski instructors could be regarded as amateurs led the Swiss and Austrian teams to boycott the alpine events.

On the speed-skating oval, Norway's Ivar Ballangrud won three of the four races. Sonja Henie of Norway secured her third consecutive Olympic title in figure skating. Switzerland won the four-man bobsled in a combined time of 5:19.85, and Norway topped the overall medal table with seven gold, five silver and three bronze medals.

The ice hockey tournament produced the Games' most contentious episode. Most of the British team had learned the game in Canada, and Canada, the reigning Olympic champion, protested the inclusion of two of them—goaltender Jimmy Foster and forward Alex Archer—on the grounds that, having joined English clubs, they should be treated as professionals. The protest was withdrawn partway through the tournament as a gesture of goodwill, a concession Canada came to regret: with Foster in goal, Great Britain beat Canada 2–1—Edgar Brenchley scored the winning goal—and took the gold medal, ending Canada's run of four consecutive Olympic titles. Because the Olympic tournament also counted as that year's World Championship, the British side became the first European team to be crowned world champions.

The medals presented at the Games were the largest and heaviest awarded at any Olympics up to that date, measuring 100 mm in diameter and 4 mm thick.

## Legacy

The 1936 Winter Games let German organisers rehearse arrangements for security, transport, communications and crowd control that were used again at the Summer Olympics in Berlin. On the strength of the event, the IOC awarded the 1940 Winter Olympics to Garmisch-Partenkirchen, although those Games were cancelled after the outbreak of the Second World War. On 7 March 1936, less than three weeks after the closing ceremony, Hitler ordered the remilitarization of the Rhineland, his first open breach of the Treaty of Versailles.
